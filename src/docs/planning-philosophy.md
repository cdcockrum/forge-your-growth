# Forge Planning Philosophy

Forge is not a task manager.

It is a deliberate-practice system designed to help people make steady, sustainable progress toward becoming who they want to become.

The weekly plan should feel intentional, achievable, and encouraging.

## Core Principles

### 1. Consistency beats intensity

A sustainable thirty-minute practice repeated throughout the week is usually more valuable than one exhausting session followed by inactivity.

Forge should favor plans users can realistically repeat.

### 2. Protect meaningful work

High-priority and demanding practices should receive suitable space rather than being placed wherever an opening happens to exist.

Deep work should not be crowded by unnecessary sessions.

### 3. Balance the week

Forge should avoid concentrating too many practices on one day while leaving other suitable days empty.

Balanced does not mean perfectly equal. It means intentionally distributed.

### 4. Respect preferences

Preferred days and scheduling preferences represent the user’s understanding of their own life.

Forge should honor them whenever possible, while still protecting the quality of the overall plan.

### 5. Respect recovery

Demanding practices should not be repeatedly stacked without adequate recovery when alternative scheduling is available.

Difficulty, physical strain, and cognitive load should influence placement.

### 6. Preserve user agency

Forge may recommend and generate, but the user remains in control.

Users should be able to add, remove, move, skip, and regenerate practices without fighting the system.

### 7. Explain important decisions

The planner should eventually be able to explain why a practice was placed on a particular day.

Examples:

- Preferred day
- Lower daily workload
- Better recovery spacing
- Balanced distribution
- Existing session preserved

### 8. Preserve history

Generating a new week must not alter previous weeks.

Regenerating a current week should preserve completed practices unless the user explicitly chooses otherwise.

### 9. Avoid guilt-driven design

A missed practice is information, not failure.

Forge should encourage reflection and adjustment rather than shame, punishment, or manipulative streak mechanics.

### 10. Plans should inspire action

The final plan should feel clear and approachable.

A technically optimal schedule that overwhelms the user is not a successful plan.

## Product Language

Forge uses language associated with growth and practice:

- Practice, not task
- Skill, not habit
- Weekly plan, not task list
- Complete, skip, move, or reflect
- Progress, not perfection

## Planning Priorities

When several days are valid, the planner should generally consider:

1. Existing sessions and completed work
2. Preferred days
3. Daily workload
4. Recovery spacing
5. Priority
6. Difficulty
7. Consistent distribution
8. Stable and predictable results

## Deterministic Before Artificial Intelligence

Forge’s core planner must work without an external AI model.

The deterministic planner should be:

- Reliable
- Testable
- Explainable
- Fast
- Safe to run repeatedly

Artificial intelligence may later improve recommendations, interpret reflections, and suggest adjustments, but it should enhance the planning engine rather than replace it.

## Guiding Question

Before adding or changing a planning feature, ask:

> Does this help the user make meaningful, sustainable progress toward becoming who they want to become?
